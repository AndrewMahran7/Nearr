# Nearr v100 Vision

Nearr is the place where people follow through on the life they keep saving.

Every day, people see restaurants, hikes, hotels, beaches, coffee shops, events, date spots, trips, shops, and experiences online. They send posts to friends, save videos, screenshot ideas, and say, “we should go.” Most of it disappears. The restaurant never gets tried. The hike never happens. The trip stays in the group chat.

Nearr turns that saved intent into real life.

Nearr is not just a social map app. It is the accountability layer between online inspiration and real-world experience.

Social media creates desire. Nearr captures it, understands it, remembers it, recommends it at the right time, and helps people actually go.

The core loop is simple:

```text
See it → Save it → Remember it → Decide → Go → Capture the experience
```

The long-term goal is for Nearr to become the place-intent layer for social media and the real-world action engine for everyday life.

---

# The Problem: Saved Life, Unlived

People are surrounded by inspiration, but most inspiration never becomes action.

A person sees a restaurant on TikTok. They save a hike from Instagram. A friend sends a hotel. A creator posts a city guide. A group chat talks about a trip. Everyone is interested, but nobody follows through.

The problem is not that people lack ideas. The problem is that their intent is scattered across social platforms, screenshots, DMs, notes, browser tabs, and memory.

Social platforms are great at creating desire, but they are not built to help users act on that desire later.

Maps are great when users already know where they want to go, but they do not remember the social context that created the intent.

Review platforms help users search, but they do not capture the places users already discovered from people they trust.

Nearr sits in the gap.

Nearr remembers what people wanted to do, understands where it is, and brings it back when it matters.

# The Final Product Thesis

Nearr becomes the system of record for real-world intent.

The app knows:

- places a user wanted to try
- where the intent came from
- which creator, friend, post, or trip sparked it
- how long the user has been putting it off
- whether the user is nearby
- whether the user has enough time to go
- whether the place fits the current context
- whether friends or creators have been there
- whether the user finally completed the experience

The highest-value version of Nearr is not a place-saving tool.

It is the layer that connects:

```text
Social content → Place intent → Real-world action → Completed experience
```

At full scale, Nearr does not just know where people go.

Nearr knows what people wanted to do before they searched, navigated, reserved, ordered, visited, or shared.

That is the strategic value.

# Core Loop

## 1. See It

The user discovers a place or experience through:

- Instagram
- TikTok
- YouTube
- X/Twitter
- Reddit
- Pinterest
- Google
- a blog
- a creator map
- a friend’s map
- a group chat
- a generic web link

The content can point to almost anything real:

- restaurants
- coffee shops
- bars
- hikes
- beaches
- hotels
- parks
- museums
- stores
- events
- neighborhoods
- date spots
- trip ideas
- local experiences

## 2. Save It

The user sends the content to Nearr.

Nearr identifies the real-world place or experience behind the content.

It understands messy social evidence:

- captions
- descriptions
- tagged accounts
- creator bios
- hashtags
- comments
- visible addresses
- location text
- video transcripts
- links in bio
- place names
- city context
- friend or creator context

The save should feel instant, but not reckless.

Nearr should only auto-save when it is confident. When evidence is ambiguous, Nearr should ask the user to choose. A clean confirmation is better than a wrong save.

## 3. Remember It

Nearr stores more than a pin.

It remembers:

- the place
- the source post
- the reason the user saved it
- the creator or friend who influenced it
- the category
- the location
- the user’s notes
- whether the user has ignored it before
- whether it belongs to a trip, group map, or creator map

Nearr should make saved intent easy to rediscover.

## 4. Decide

Nearr helps the user answer:

> What should I actually do right now?

The decision engine can consider:

- current location
- saved places nearby
- density of saved spots in an area
- time of day
- day of week
- how long the user has been putting something off
- whether the user is traveling
- how much time the user has
- category fit
- previous saves
- previous completions
- friends’ activity
- creator influence
- weather or seasonality when relevant
- whether the place is part of a larger plan

Nearr should recommend from the user’s own intent first.

It should not feel like generic discovery. It should feel like Nearr is resurfacing things the user already wanted.

## 5. Go

Nearr should make action obvious.

The app should support:

- get directions
- open in Apple Maps or Google Maps
- start a route
- call
- reserve
- order
- invite friends
- add to a plan
- mark as visited
- skip for now
- archive if the user is no longer interested

The jump from “saved” to “I’m going” should feel natural.

## 6. Capture the Experience

After the user goes, Nearr should let them complete the loop.

They can:

- mark the place as visited
- add a photo
- write a note
- rate the experience privately
- share it to a map
- show followers that they went
- contribute to a group trip memory
- complete a creator map stop

Photos in Nearr are not meant to create another addictive feed.

They are proof of experience.

Nearr should help users build a record of the life they actually lived, not just the life they saved.

# The Social-to-Place Engine

Nearr’s foundation is the ability to understand real-world places from messy online content.

At full scale, Nearr should be able to take almost any social link or web link and understand the real-world intent behind it.

This requires a platform-agnostic social evidence system that can interpret:

- post metadata
- captions
- creator descriptions
- tagged accounts
- creator bios
- comments
- transcripts
- visible addresses
- text overlays
- hashtags
- links
- place candidates
- city and region hints

The goal is not just to parse links.

The goal is to answer:

> What real-world place or experience is this content trying to make someone want?

The strongest technical moat is not one model call. It is the system that turns noisy social content into structured place intent.

The model can propose. The product decides what is safe.

Wrong saves break trust. Nearr should fail safely when the evidence is weak, ask when there are multiple possible places, and save automatically only when confidence is strong.

# The Accountability Engine

Nearr is not only a memory app. It is an accountability system for real-world enjoyment.

The accountability should feel supportive, not annoying.

Nearr should help users follow through by asking:

- What have you been meaning to try?
- What are you near right now?
- What have you been putting off?
- What places are clustered nearby?
- What would make sense with the time you have?
- What did your friends or favorite creators recommend?
- What trip, list, or map are you close to completing?

The app should nudge users toward action without making them feel guilty.

Examples:

- “You’re near 3 places you saved.”
- “You saved this last month and you’re 8 minutes away.”
- “This would complete your Santa Cruz coffee map.”
- “Two friends added this to the group map.”
- “You have 90 minutes free and this hike is nearby.”
- “You keep saving ramen spots. This one is close.”

Nearr should not spam users.

The best reminder is one that feels like the user would have wanted it if they remembered the place themselves.

# The Recommendation Engine

Nearr’s recommendations should be based on intent, not generic popularity.

A typical recommendation app asks:

> What is popular near you?

Nearr should ask:

> What did you already want to do, and what makes sense right now?

The recommendation system should consider:

- saved places
- ignored saves
- completed experiences
- nearby density
- age of saved intent
- category preferences
- time of day
- travel context
- creator maps followed
- friends’ maps
- group plans
- previous actions
- current location
- distance and travel time
- whether the user tends to complete similar experiences

A powerful recommendation might not be the highest-rated restaurant nearby.

It might be the taco place the user saved 47 days ago, passed three times, and keeps forgetting to try.

That is what makes Nearr different.

Nearr does not need to invent desire from scratch.

Nearr helps users act on desire they already expressed.

# Monetization at Scale

Nearr should be free for regular users.

Regular users should not have to pay to remember places, follow through on experiences, or use the core product.

Nearr should also avoid traditional ads that pollute the map or break user trust.

The business model should come from the businesses, creators, and partners that benefit when online intent becomes offline action.

## 1. Privacy-Safe Intent Intelligence

Nearr’s most valuable business asset is real-world intent.

But that data must be handled carefully.

Nearr should not sell individual user behavior, personal location trails, or creepy user-level data.

Instead, Nearr can provide aggregated, privacy-safe insights such as:

- how many people saved a restaurant
- which creators drove saves
- which places generated directions
- which neighborhoods are gaining intent
- which types of experiences are trending
- how many saves converted into visits
- which campaigns created real-world action

A restaurant should be able to know:

> “150 people saved us from social content this month, 42 opened directions, and 19 marked us visited.”

A restaurant should not be able to know:

> “This specific person was nearby and ignored us.”

Trust is part of the moat.

## 2. Business Attribution

Businesses care about customers, not likes.

Nearr can help restaurants, hotels, venues, shops, and local businesses understand how online interest turns into real-world action.

Nearr can show:

- saves
- reminder opens
- direction taps
- visit completions
- creator influence
- map inclusion
- campaign performance
- local demand trends

This gives businesses a clearer picture of demand before the transaction happens.

## 3. Creator Tools

Creators can use Nearr as a business tool.

Potential creator monetization includes:

- premium creator maps
- analytics dashboards
- attribution reports
- branded city guides
- business partnership tools
- paid map placements with clear labeling
- revenue share from campaigns
- creator subscription tiers for serious operators

Creators should pay only when Nearr helps them make money, prove influence, or manage their recommendations more professionally.

## 4. Sponsored Experiences, Not Traditional Ads

Businesses may eventually pay for placement, but Nearr must protect the user experience.

Paid placement should never feel like random ads dropped onto a personal map.

Sponsored experiences should be:

- relevant
- clearly labeled
- based on user intent
- aligned with saved categories
- useful in the moment
- easy to dismiss
- never allowed to override organic trust

Nearr should monetize intent without corrupting it.

## 5. Local Partnerships

Nearr can eventually support partnerships with:

- restaurants
- hotels
- tourism boards
- universities
- event organizers
- local governments
- neighborhood associations
- travel companies
- reservation platforms
- delivery platforms
- rideshare platforms

The common thread is real-world action.

Nearr becomes valuable to any partner that wants to understand or influence what people decide to do in the physical world.
